Digital Entertainment

The Saga of Sideshow Bob

As every right-thinking person knows, video games are cool, and arcades rock. Even supposing that the actual games themselves that one finds in an arcade can't compete on a technological level with, say, the latest PC games or a console, there is just something primordially tactile about a physical piece of equipment, dedicated to gaming, that has a certain intrinsic honor.

Physical arcades are public places, and the experience of gathering with your buddies to have fun and play games, and perhaps challenge all comers to best you in digital combat, is one not to be missed.

For these reasons, Zteve and I begat a project to build our own arcade cabinet. Well, actually to build me an arcade cabinet since Zteve doesn't have room for one of his own. The general idea is to build a piece of furniture into which one places a PC and a suitable display device (i.e. monitor or TV) and use it to play games.

We can play anything from the latest first-person shooter, to classic arcade games. Really, this creature is just an homage to the Arcade Experience and simply adds a certain style and invokes a certain type of feeling for the room it's placed in.

Anyway, that's all bullshit justification. The fact is we don't need any fscking "justification", so we're just doing it. So there.

This site documents the development of Project: Sideshow Bob.

Sideshow Bob is so named because it is a "sideshow" to Project X74, the game that Steve and I are developing. I think it's turned out to be just as big and interesting as PX74, though.

Chapter 2: Proof of Concept

Selecting Parts

Controlling ourselves

First we needed actual arcade parts. So, I ordered an "Ultimate Joystick", and 8 "ultimate Pushbuttons" from happ controls. That got us started.

Getting Input

USB or not USB? That is the question.

Your average "game pad" is just a small circuit board, with "E"-shaped contacts on the board, whose fingers interlace. When you mash on a button, a conducting plate shorts those fingers, and the game pad registers a button press.

Here's a typical game pad. pretty straightforward. Here's that same game pad gutted. Here's that same board, from the other side. You can probably make out the points on the board where the buttons would go.

With this knowledge, I started to get down to business. I picked up one of those controllers just mentioned (obviously,) and soldered leads from the joystick's microswitches to the points on the board. I suck at soldering, so this was a little something of a challenge for me. Eventually, though, I did get it to work.

Now, electrically this worked fine. However, a guy at work who is actually, like, an electrical engineer and has been doing this stuff since the dawn of time warned me to be sure that the soldering connections were "physically able" to withstand the strain. I thought he was worried about having the connections pop off the board. I was mistaken.

No, he was actually worried about the contact pads physically tearing off the board, because the solder clings to the pads harder than the pads cling to the boards. Thusly was the P150 lost to me. I could probably have salvaged it, but a better solution presented itself anyway.

Unfortunately, I didn't get any photos of the P150 wired to the joystick.

A Better Solution

Enter the IPAC

At this point I figured it was time to check out this ipac thing I'd heard about. Basically, it does everything you could conceivably need it to do, and comes with a terminal block to which you attach wires. It just doesn't get any simpler than that. it just doesn't damnit!

So, I ordered an IPAC. wiring the joystick to it was trivially easy, especially since I'd already soldered up the joystick for the P150, anyway.

Once I had the IPAC working, it was time to move along, and actually start building a prototype.

Chapter 3: Prototype

Control Panelling

A joyful stick

What's an arcade cabinet without large, shiny, candy-like buttons to press that respond with viscerally satisfying tactile feedback? Indeed, it is naught without them, and so it was time to wire some buttons up to the IPAC too.

The problem with that is that you really can't wire up buttons until you've mounted them into some kind of panel. That meant it was time Zteve and I hit the Home Despot and grabbed ourselves some wood-like substance to start hacking up.

We picked up some medium-density fiberboard (MDF) and then prepared a layout for the buttons. Basically we did this by just putting my hand on a piece of paper, marking where my fingers naturally laid, and then centering the buttons on those points. This produced two "arcs" of buttons instead of rows. This might sound strage, but there are actually arcade cabinets that have this configuration. We copied this template onto a piece of paper, and prepared to start making holes.

Zteve brought his tools, and away we went. first we drilled holes for the joystick. The stick actually includes official dimensions for the stick, so we weren't (entirely) shooting in the dark on that.

Pushing My Buttons

A sextuplet of holes

Next, we just took a copy of the button layout template, and taped it to the MDF in what seemed like a decent spot, relative to the joystick. Zteve then drilled through the template directly into the board. The paper actually shredded pretty well, so it was fortunate that we had the foresight to also mark the center points of the buttons with small nail-marks.

After drilling the holes, we installed the buttons.

Common Ground

An iron hand

Since Zteve had not had the "pleasure" of working with a soldering iron recently, I let him make the connections between the microswitches on the joystick and buttons. (Those wires then just screw into the IPAC terminal block.)

here's a picture of zteve's handiwork. You can also see the IPAC in that photo, connected to the keyboard and PC as well.

Lessons Learned

Boy, did we botch this

We actually learned quite a bit from this experience. Here are some random notes, in no particular order. We will be using these as input into the next step, which is an actual attempt to build the control box. The rest of the cabinet will follow.

  • The joystick should probably be rotated about 8 degrees or so clockwise, for more comfort. This means it won't be "square" to the way the user is facing the cabinet, but it will be more comfortable to use. We'll just put some kind of marking on the cabinet's surface to inform new users of this fact.
  • We didn't actually need to solder anything. In fact, we should probably NOT solder anything. The IPAC already accepts its connections via a terminal block, and the buttons' and joystick's switches have flat tabs as connectors. This means that we should be able to use those flat "fast-attach connectors" (as I believe Radio Shack calls them) to make installation easier, and make replacing parts MUCH easier. This means we'll have to undo a non-trivial (but not huge) amount of soldering. Oh well.
  • It turned out when we installed the buttons that we laid them out so that they just barely fit next to each other. However, we didn't realize that the screw-on "nut" that secures the button to the board from underneath is wider than the top of the buttons! As a result, it holds together okay for now, but we are going to have to spread the buttons out a bit on the final copy. (In the photo above, you can especially see this with the red buttons.)
  • We used flat-head screws to secure the joystick. This was a good start; however, we need to recess them. Unfortunately we had no counter-sink bit, so right now they are not. This will be cool, because with recessed flat-head screws, we can lay the contact paper right over the top of the panel, and the screws won't even be visible at all. In most cabinets I've seen, the screw heads are either visible, or even worse, and look like they might get irritating after a while.

So, the prototype did exactly what it was supposed to do: it taught us some interesting details. Now we feel well-equipped to translate this information to the "final" (or at least, first cut of the final version) of the control panel.

Even now, it's already supremely cool. It's fun to just use the prototype board instead of a joystick. Here's Zteve testing the board with Galaga.

Updating the Wiring

Rectifying mistakes

As mentioned earlier, the initial prototype assembly of the control panel involved soldering the joints up. This is the hard way to do things, since it makes it difficult to disassemble the panel if you need to replace parts.

So, I spent some time and (with some difficulty) unsoldered all the joints and got the solder largely cleaned off the connector tabs. Then I had to go to the local Ripoff Shack and locate some fast-disconnect tab terminal connectors. After a couple false starts and another trip to obtain a suitable crimping tool, I was able to wire everything up with the new connectors.

you can see the results here. This technique is highly recommended, and it works well. I tested the re-wired panel, and it continues to work fine. The only other comment I have is be sure to leave yourself plenty of wire when you hook these things together; a couple times I left myself pretty tight in terms of space, and had to splice a couple wires as you can see in the photo. Eventually I just began erring on the side of excess, which I recommend. You might lose a little wire, but it's better than screwing up a whole chain because you left a single segment too short. (This, obviously, only applies to the common ground bus wire. The actual signal leads are point-to-point and hard to screw up.)

Getting there

After a hiatus of some weeks (due to various illnesses, vacations, and holidays on Steve's and my parts,) we finally made some good progress this week. We're not quite configuration complete, but we're getting there. The following sections summarize our status.

ARSE-Monster Steve

A true software virtuoso

Steve has taken our rough design for ARSE and actually implemented a prototype. Here's his status, in his own words.

ARSE 0.00001

As Dan had mentioned before, pretty much every arcade ROM selector we found was either too presumptuous or severely broken. By presumpuous I mean wanting keyboard and mouse and usually, GTK. By severely broken I mean, well, even after massaging the Makefile and fiddling with bits of code, completely non-operational.

So, on a few scraps of paper, ARSE was born. (Gotta hand it to Dan for the bitchin' acronym...) We were guided by two basic principles, simple interface and no GUI.

You want a simple interface first off for ease of use. It shouldn't take more than a few taps on the joystick to get to the game you want to play. Second of all, our input options are severely limited, basically just the joystick and a few buttons.

As for the GUI, Sideshow Bob's guts consist of a fairly stripped down RedHat install, no room for big fancy window managers. (No ARSE does NOT need to do transparency...) However, I happen to be a big fan of ncurses. I think it's fun to work with and it produces some decent looking output.

With those two thoughts in mind, the plan for ARSE came together. We'd have two areas on the screen. The top is the input area. It contains a set of alphanumeric characters that can be selected using a joystick controlled cursor. Think of how you enter your initials into an arcade game. (Tap, tap, tap, "S", tap, tap, "E", tap, tap, tap, tap "Z"...) This allows you to spell out the first few letter of the game name you want to play.

The second area is a large, scrollable list of the games. As you select letters in the input area, ARSE appropriately selects the ROMs whose names match the key from the input area. I've found that with the current list of MAME ROMS (about 3600), it only takes about 3 characters before you're down to something less than 20 ROMS. This makes it much easier to select the ROM you want to play. So, with a little joystick jockeying and about 5 or 6 button presses, you're at your favorite game.

The Prototype

With this in mind, Dan and I broke up the work. I would work on the ROM sorting/selection piece while he worked on the ncurses layout. Dan scored first and got a basic ncurses app running that presented two windows on the screen. (And even printed "Hello" in them...what a codewizard) This was checked into the ARSE CVS repository for my perusal. A few days later, I started hacking together a basic ROM sorter. Lemme tell you, I believe in the STL now more than ever. The rom_sort program just parsed a text file of ROM names, checked the path to the available ROMs, threw them in a vector, and away you go. After some user defined sort routines, I had a program that could take in a key and down-select to the set of ROMs that started with that key.

Everything was going great, well, until I got mono. That required me to sit around the house in a feverish stupor for about 2 weeks. However, at times, I did get the hankering to play with some ncurses, so I stole Dan's part and started building up the ncurses interface. I took his base and started throwing together the prototype. After a few hours of coding, I had a functional ncurses interface that let you punch in rom names and switch between input and list areas.

So, after I recovered, Dan and I resumed our regular SSB meetings. At the last one, I finally bit the bullet and frankensteined the rom_sort code with the arse prototype interface. Amazingly enough, after a few unholy acts of code (which I won't go into being an embarrased software engineer) ARSE Version 0.00001 works. It allows a user to type in a ROM name and select from a ROM list that matches the input key. I just need to add an spawn() statement to fire off an appropriate UNIX command.

Enhancements to the prototype will include only displaying the ROMs that the user actually has (in the roms/ directory) and making a properties file so a user can tailor ARSE to their system. But so far, so good.

Miscellania

Driving me insania

Once we got the TV, computer, ARSE, and MAME largely working together, details started presenting themselves to us.

First, we realized that we need to somehow turn the TV on and off with the system. TVs aren't monitors and so don't do things like Energy Star DPMS power management. Consequently we need a way for the PC to turn the TV on during its boot cycle. (We could always just keep the remote handy and do it ourselves, but that's a cop-out.) Ultimately we decided to go buy a cheap universal remote and hack it up, so that we can trigger its "power" button via a signal sent from the parallel port to a transistor wired to the power button's contacts inside the remote. That still remains to be done.

Another thorny related problem is how we turn the PC itself on and off. The problem is that it is an ATX system, meaning that when you shut the system down it turns off the power supply, and the only way to turn it back on is to hit the power button. This is difficult if the PC is inside a big wooden cabinet. Initially we'd intended to try and disable or bypass ATX and control power externally with a power strip and use a "big red button" wired to the interrupt-generating pin of the parallel port to shut down the system. However, we realized that we could just invoke shutdowns from within ARSE, which is much cleaner. Moreover, Mike then pointed out that there is a pair of pins on the motherboard that the case power switch connects to, and that we could simply replace the case power switch with an external button. I then wired up a standard arcade button to these 2 pins on the motherboard, and it works perfectly. We will simply install this "Big Red Button" on the exterior of the cabinet, say on the side somewhere.

The IPAC happily does chording, which makes it easy for us to hit the ESCAPE key to bomb out of MAME back to ARSE. However, we discovered that we can't remap ARSE to use the same keycodes that the IPAC and MAME use by default, because some of them are keydown/keyup events on meta characters (such as left control) that ncurses simply doesn't ever see. So, Steve is going to remap keys and then we'll have to tweak the IPAC and MAME accordingly.

Once we have the remote control issue working, all we will have to do is build the cabinet. However, then we have to paint and otherwise decorate the cabinet -- notably, we'll need art for the marquee on the cabinet. This was a bit of a pickle, since neither Steve nor I has much artistic talent. Fortunately, Aimee's Mom works as a graphic designer, and she kindly agreed to help us out! so here's a big shout-out to aimee's mom! Sideshow Bob lives!

None of the issues above prevent us from having a functional system; they're mostly just convenience. As a result, I am declaring Sideshow Bob officially configuration complete!

Chapter 7: Cabinet Construction Begins

Making Dreams a Reality...

...with power tools

Steve and I had an All-Holy Build the Arcade Cabinet, Already Weekend™ yesterday and today. We started out by reviewing the plans we got from lusid and coming up with a task order of assembly and working out some of the smaller details, like precisely how we are going to mount the control box onto the cabinet, etc. We went through a bunch of options, such as hinges, such as hinges, carriage bolts, cleats, removable screws, etc., but finally settled on these details:

  • Due to weight and materials availability considerations, we are using 5/8" particle board instead of MDF to build the bulk of the cabinet; the control panel top is still MDF for "texture" reasons
  • Particle board doesn't paint quite as well as MDF, so we will be using a deep glossy latex-based paint for the cabinet.
  • the control panel box must be removable; otherwise the finished cabinet will be 34" inches wide which is too wide to fit through many doorways. (The cabinet excluding the control panel is only 27.5" wide.)
  • The control panel box will be screwed down onto a 2x4 segment mounted in the cabinet (vs. screwed into the ends of the particle board.
  • The monitor support inside the cabinet on which the TV will rest will be supported by 2x4 segments forming cleats or rails on which most of the TV's weight will rest (vs. screwing the ends of the support directly into the walls of the cabinet, which would be less strong.)
  • The top of the control panel will be attached to the rest of the control box via steel L-brackets mounted at the corners. (We considered hinging the top and including locking brackets of some kind, but eventually abandoned that since it was a lot of work/silliness for something that you're not even going to do that often.)
  • The construction of the bulk of the cabinet will be done by biscuit joints so as to avoid any visible external hardware (i.e. screws) and for durability.
  • T-molding will be used on all visible edges of the cabinet except the control panel, which will be rounded-edged via router for comfort.

Otherwise, we are following LuSiD's plans pretty closely. Steve has an AutoCAD drawing of the cabinet which we are treating as the official plans, and are working from paper while down in the dusty basement.

Let It Begin

Sawdust is afoot

Once we had the plans worked out, we started the actual construction. Essentially, the design requires around two and a half 4'x8' sheets of 5/8" wood (for which we are using particle board.) However, we needed to buy 3 full sheets since there is a bunch of scrap that you end up with after you cut out pieces. Since we decided to do the control panel top with 1/2" MDF (so that we can router the edge), we actually have quite a bit more extra particle board area than LuSiD's plans would normally have yielded us. That was nice since it provided us a very comfortable amount of breathing room.

Construction began in my dad's basement. He has a very nice selection of tools, two of which are highly recommended if not imperative for a project like this: a circular saw, and a table saw. If you don't have a table saw you can probably get away with a circular saw, but you really want a table saw if you can at all get one; it makes life much, much easier.

So far we've cut up two of the 4' x 8' sheets, and have a pile of parts. In the next few days, we'll cut up the last sheet, and then begin assembling the parts. Assembly will most likely take us some time, because we'll be using biscuited joints for strength; it will certainly take us longer than it might have if we were using screws. You can't rush quality, I guess.

One thing that's become apparent is that this fscker is going to be heavy. It's not as heavy as it would have been with MDF, but it's not going to be that much lighter either. This is not the kind of thing that you're going to want to move around on a whim. Besides that, we ended up deciding not to make it "disassembleable." I simply don't forsee myself ever taking it apart, even to move; I'll take the TV and computer out, of course, but I don't see how dismantling the cabinet really buys you that much. Hopefully, though, using the particle board and thinner 5/8" material will keep things manageable.

Other Updates

Unannounced progress

There are a few other issues that haven't yet been discussed here. I'll take care of them now.

We have altered the scheme for remote control/powering on the TV again. We realized that using a universal remote -- even one hacked to take power from an external source and with the power button wired to the parallel port, etc. -- was just too clumsy to use reliably. We looked around for a more robust solution and found the lirc project. That site includes a schematic for building an infrared transmitter circuit, which we will use with LIRC to turn on the TV directly from the host computer, using the DTR signal of the RS232 port. We intend to essentially tape an IR LED on top of the IR sensor on the TV, with the rest of the circuit wired to the serial port. It should be quite cool; no intermediate remote required.

Steve has been working diligently on ARSE, and has improvements that I haven't yet installed on the SSB hardware. I need to do that soon.

We are planning to put together a "distribution" of everything SSB-related (probably on SourceForge) for others to read and perhaps make use of. This will likely included a detailed measured drawing of our plans, Steve's AutoCAD model, system configuration data (e.g. my X configuration, init scripts, etc.), and ARSE.

It's worthwhile to discuss some construction notes, and a few snafus we ran into. Here they are in no particular order.

  • We cut the t-molding slot before we assembled the cabinet, since it would be unwieldy (at best) to try and do a thing like that after we'd already assembled the thing. However, it wasn't until we were well into that I suddenly realized that the t-molding slot and biscuit slots intersect one another. That's a major problem, since the t-molding and biscuits obtruct each other.

    We can only conclude that no one else has done a biscuit installation with these plans, or at least no one that we know of. At any rate, our plan is to assemble the cabinet, and then do a quick clean-up pass with the router to re-cut the t-molding slot. Since we will be taking out material from the biscuits, we are now slightly worried about the structural integrity of the case, with compromised biscuits.

  • To ameliorate the compromised-biscuit issue, we installed glue-blocks in the interior corners of the cabinet. A glue-block is simply a piece of scrap wood that you glue on two sides and secure into the inside of a corner to increase strength. We also secured the blocks with metal brads as well as glue.
  • We have not yet installed the monitor shelf and speaker shelf. We are going to handle that last, securing them from the outside with either screws or brads. We just think it will be easier to do that (since it's already going to be tricky enough) without having to worry about biscuiting the things, having to deal with the rest of the cabinet not being stable, etc.

Monitor Shelf
Because the TV is pretty heavy, we elected to augment LuSiD's original design (which called for simply nailing the monitor shelf to the sides of the cabinet) with a stiffener H-style arrangement for the shelf to sit on. We think this is just better structurally since it should prevent the shelf from sagging over time. To secure it, we glued and brad-nailed the H-structure together, positioned it at the desired angle (which we determined to be between 11 and 25 degrees for best viewing) according to taste, and then glued and brad-nailed the monitor shelf on top of it.

We also traced out the footprint of the TV onto the monitor shelf before installing it. After getting the physical measurements of the TV (i.e. height at the front and back) we were able to verify that it all would fit into the cabinet before we permanently nailed or glued anything; it would suck to find that the TV won't fit in after we've finished building the creature.

As a final comment, we chose the viewing angle of the TV (i.e. the slope of the monitor shelf) according to two criteria: how it looks while standing, and while sitting. I intend to use this thing with a couple 30" high bar stools, so it needs to be playable in both configurations. We put the TV probably somewhere around 15 - 20 degrees off vertical.

Control Panel
The next step was to lay out the controls on the control panel. This was what we refer to -- in highly technical jargon -- as a "pain in the ass." Basically it's not only detailed and tedious, it's subjective. You have to be very careful to get it right the first time, because if you get it wrong you have to do it all over again, and there are 26 holes you have to drill and a ton of measurements to make before drilling to make one of these.

To position the hardware, we first computed the measurements of the interior of the control box, and drew those lines on top of the control panel top as a sort of "out of bounds" line, so that we had a visual cue as to how much real estate we actually have to work with. Then we cut out two pieces of paper on which we drew the center points of the control hardware -- i.e. it marked the positions of buttons and the stick. We laid out the buttons and stick for one player's "station" on the paper, then traced it to a second piece of paper, and then slid those around on the control panel top until we were happy with positioning.

Once we had the papers in place, we made sure they were symmetrically placed and then taped them down. We marked the center-points of the various holes to be drilled onto the wood itself by whacking the point of a nail through the paper. After that, we just drilled.

Button Layout Tips
The trick to laying out the buttons is to know the minimum distance apart you can place them without having their rings underneath collide. This turns out to be 1 3/8". You could just line up the buttons in a straight-up grid, separating them by 1 3/8", but we wanted a more natural layout, with the center buttons slightly above the other four, to match the natural lengths of your fingers.

When we installed the buttons, we used this trick to make a template:

  1. draw a line about 4 inches long with a straight-edge
  2. draw a line perpendicular to the first line, about a half-inch in from the left
  3. draw a second line parallel to the first line (and perpendicular to the second line) 1 3/8" above the first line
  4. on the bottom line, draw a point about an inch or so away from the vertical line; this is the center point of the lower-left button
  5. draw a point directly above the first point, but on the top line; this is the top-left button
  6. measure in 2 7/8" from the first (bottom-left) line and make a second point; this is the bottom-right button
  7. directly above that point on the top line, make another mark; this is the top-right button
  8. bisect the two parallel lines, and draw a second vertical line along the bisection
  9. measure up this new line 1/4" and make a mark; this is the bottom-middle button
  10. measure up 1/4" along the midline from the top line, and make the final mark for the top-middle button

Those steps will get you a decent map for the button layout. It'll be clearer once you do the drawing; it's really quite simple. After you've positioned the buttons on the paper, you can position the center-point for the control stick to taste. If you screw it up, just start over with a new sheet of paper -- that's why you use paper instead of drawing on the wood.

Making Cuts
Once you have everything laid out and marked, you just have to drill the holes. We recommend taking this very slowly, and being very cautious; "Measure twice, cut once" as Norm Abram says. We quickly got tired of this; it is pretty tedious to drill 26 holes in succession, carefully measuring each one. This is probably the trickiest part of the whole cabinet-making operation, though, so stay the course and make sure you get a quality product.

Control Stick Notes
The four holes for the control sticks' bolts were drilled with a counter-sink bit so that flat-head screw-bolts can be used to secure the things while maintaining a perfectly flat playing surface. We actually did these after laying out and drilling everything else, because we weren't sure how to lay them out. There are basically two choice: machine-oriented or player-oriented. The difference is this: when you expect the players to stand at a slight angle to the machine and put the buttons at that angle, do you have the left-right axis of the stick be aligned with the angle the player stands at (i.e. the player's right is the stick's right) or aligned with the cabinet itself (i.e. the game's right is the stick's right.) This is one for the ages, and is a matter of taste. We chose player-oriented controls.

Final Notes
Once everything else was done, we drilled the four holes along the top for the Player 1/Player 2 coin/start buttons by positioning them 1" in from the top "out of bounds" line, and spacing them 2" apart from each other.

The only thing remaining to be done is that we have to drill a hole in the side of the box (under the lip of the panel top) for the button that will be wired to the ATX header on the computer motherboard. We already drilled the hole through the back of the case for the PS/2 keyboard cable that will connect the IPAC to the computer.

There isn't much else to say; painting isn't exactly rocket science. There are a few comments we can make, though.

First, a quick note on how we attached the control box to the cabinet. Over the course of all this, we considered everything from hinges to L-brackets to elaborate hook-and-clasp mechanisms, but in the end settled on something quite simple. As you can see in the first photo above, we just attached blocks to the bottom of the control box top (using brads from above, and glue) and then simply insert those blocks into the box structure, and then screw through the sides of the box into the blocks. (The box itself attaches to the cabinet via a similar technique.) This is easily accomplished by simply screwing the blocks into the side of the control box first, and then (carefully) bradding and gluing the panel onto the blocks, and then unscrewing the blocks for removal. That way you have a good solid fit. It turns out that we probably don't even need to screw the box into the cabinet, since friction holds it so well on its own. The panel top will have to be screwed in place, though, since there is some lateral play front-to-back.

We did use quite a few brads on this little creation. We had assumed that when we painted it, the brad marks would be invisible. It turns out that they're not. By carefully applying paint to at least reduce visibility, we have something we're happy with, but the marks are still visible if you look. If we were to do it again we would try and fill them with wood putty, as we did the screw holes for the monitor shelf. However, it's not a major defect.

Otherwise, that's pretty much it. (This will be a small block of wood on the floor of the monitor shelf that the TV rests against.) We also have to whip out a little gaffer's tape to cover over a few gaps between boards on the back, and prevent light leakage. These are very minor, however.

There really isn't a great deal more to say. I've already commented on the wiring, the stools, and the cabinet. This was literally just putting it all together. There are a few things that are worth mentioning though.

First, the paint. I chose an eggshell/satin finish for the paint, primarily because of the texture it provides. Indeed, the texture is excellent and does feel like an actual arcade cabinet. However, it's standard wall paint, and is not very durable. It got rather easily scuffed in the move. Also, that finish is still somewhat porous, making it feel tacky on the control panel surface. Were I to do this again, I would absolutely choose to use an enamel finish, and as durable a paint as I can find.

The only other thing to mention is the aluminum molding at the bottom. Steve and my dad strongly recommended this as a way to protect the bottom edges so that they don't chip too badly, especially at the toe kick plate area. I didn't think that was necessary, but it can't hurt and I agreed that it might also look nice. In point of fact, it looks fantastic, and I highly recommend using it. In fact, if I were to do it again I might look for even more tasteful places to put it. Another alternative might be to have used the shiny "chromed" t-molding that you can get, instead of the black. That might also have looked awesome.
